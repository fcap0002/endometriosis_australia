What is it, and who does it affect?

Endometriosis is a chronic inflammatory condition affecting women across all ages — but it most commonly emerges during the years women are at school, building careers, and starting families.

Common symptoms

Symptoms vary widely between individuals, which is part of why diagnosis takes so long.

The Burden of Living with Endometriosis

Endometriosis is rarely fatal, but it quietly drains years from one.

Years of life, not years lost

Endometriosis is not fatal. Australian women still live, on average, 85 years. However, the pain, fatigue, and limitations is absorbed quietly into ordinary days by those living with the condition.

Source: AIHW Burden of Disease Study 2024; ABS Life Expectancy Data 2021-2023

03

Where you live matters

The burden of endometriosis is not shared evenly across Australia. Hospitalisation rates vary by more than two-fold between states and territories - a pattern shaped partly by access to specialist care, partly by cross-border patient flows, and partly by the simple fact that diagnosis depends on who is looking.

04

Treatment and Procedures

Endometriosis is diagnosed surgically, treated surgically, and often re-treated surgically. What happens in the operatin room and how long women wait to get there defines much of the experience of the disease.

Access depends on who pays

Endometriosis hospitalisations are disproportionately privately funded. Across all female hospitalisations, 32% are covered by private health insurance. For endometriosis, that figure jumps to 57%. This means thtat women who can afford private care are more likely to get faster access to diagnosis and treatment, while those relying on public healthcare face longer waits and more uncertainty.

Source: AIHW National Elective Surgery Waiting Times Database, 2021-2022 (Table 1a)

05

The economic burden

Endometriosis costs the Australian health system around $247 million each year — and the money flows almost entirely through hospitals, not the early-stage care where diagnosis might happen sooner.
